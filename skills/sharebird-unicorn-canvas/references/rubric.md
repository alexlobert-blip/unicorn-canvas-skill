# Unicorn Canvas — Pressure-Test Rubric

Per-layer rubric for the Unicorn Messaging Framework canvas. Each section has:

- **Job** — what this layer accomplishes in one sentence.
- **Common mistake** — the failure mode that shows up most.
- **Pressure-test questions** — the dialogue prompts to push back with.

Tone: direct, specific, never preachy. Push back on weak answers but never block. If the user pushes back on a pressure-test, accept their reasoning and move on — your job is to surface the weakness, not litigate it.

---

## Brand line

**Job.** State, in one sentence, who the company is in the customer's world. Repeatable from memory. Customer-facing language only.

**Common mistake.** Writing a category descriptor ("we're a data security platform") instead of a customer-world line ("we keep your data discoverable, governed, and out of the wrong hands"). The first describes a product shelf. The second describes a customer outcome.

**Pressure-test questions.**
1. Would a customer say this sentence back to you in their own words after a 30-min call? If not, it is too internal.
2. Strip every product word from this line. Does it still make sense? If yes, the line is too generic — swap one product anchor back in.
3. Does this line work when read aloud at a sales kickoff and on a website hero? If it only works in one place, it is too narrow.
4. If a competitor copied this line word-for-word, would it still be true of them? If yes, it is undifferentiated.
5. Is this a sentence, or is it a tagline? A brand line is a sentence — taglines are downstream.

## Pains

**Job.** Surface the three customer pains the company is uniquely qualified to solve. Mutually exclusive. The first pain is dominant — the others ladder under it.

**Common mistake.** Picking three pains that all collapse into the same root pain ("data is messy" / "data is hard to find" / "data is hard to govern"). These are the same pain stated three ways.

**Pressure-test questions.**
1. Could a customer experience Pain 1 without experiencing Pain 2? If no, they are the same pain — merge or re-cut.
2. Which one is dominant? If you cannot pick one, the three are not yet cut correctly.
3. Are these pains a customer would say out loud, or are they internal department names? "Access governance" is internal. "Auditors are flagging our access controls" is what a customer says.
4. If you ran a survey asking your three target ICPs to rank these pains, would the rank order be consistent across ICPs? If no, you may have three pains for three different ICPs — which is a different framework.
5. Do these pains map to real budget? A pain without budget is a wish list item.

## Differentiation

**Job.** For each pain, name the one customer-value-shaped differentiator that makes this company the answer.

**Common mistake.** Filling the row with capability words ("we have AI", "we have agents", "we have a graph") instead of customer-value words ("catches data exfiltration in minutes instead of weeks", "auto-revokes stale access without breaking workflows").

**Pressure-test questions.**
1. Read this aloud. Is the subject the customer's outcome, or your product's feature? If it is your product's feature, rewrite it.
2. Would three competitors write this exact same sentence about their own product? If yes, it is a category claim, not a differentiator.
3. What would a skeptical CISO say after reading this? If the answer is "so what", the customer value is missing.
4. Does the language ladder up to the pain in the same column? If you cannot draw a line from the pain to the differentiation, the column is broken.
5. If you removed every adjective ("blazing-fast", "AI-native", "enterprise-grade"), is there still a real claim underneath? If not, the differentiation is decoration.

## Outcomes

**Job.** For each pain, name the numeric, customer-facing, real-reference outcome the company has delivered.

**Common mistake.** Vague directional language ("faster", "more secure", "easier") with no number and no named reference. Or numbers without a customer story behind them (a benchmark, not a result).

**Pressure-test questions.**
1. Is there a number? If no, this is not yet an outcome.
2. Is the number customer-facing (something the buyer will feel) or internal (something only engineering will feel)? "Cut investigation time from 14 days to 90 minutes" is customer-facing. "Reduced query latency 40%" is internal.
3. Is there a named reference behind this number — a real customer who would let you say their name? If no, the outcome is unaudited.
4. Does the outcome resolve the pain in the same column? If the pain is "auditors are flagging us" and the outcome is "engineers ship faster", the column is misaligned.
5. Could a competitor cite the same number? If yes, the outcome is generic — find the version of this story that is uniquely yours.

## Modules

**Job.** For each pain, name three product modules or capabilities that ladder up to it. SKU-shaped — what shows up on a pricing page.

**Common mistake.** Listing marketing themes ("AI-powered insights", "intelligent automation") instead of SKUs ("Data Discovery", "Access Reviews", "DLP Posture Manager"). Or listing modules from the wrong pain's column — modules drift to where the team is proudest of the product, not where the pain actually lives.

**Pressure-test questions.**
1. Could a buyer order this on a contract? If no, it is not a module — it is a marketing theme.
2. Are these three modules the ones that genuinely solve the pain in this column, or are they the three modules your team is currently most proud of? If the latter, re-cut by pain.
3. Do these modules overlap with the modules in another column? Some overlap is fine (a platform). Total overlap means the columns are not cut by pain.
4. If a sales rep walks into a deal driven by this pain, would these be the three modules they demo? If no, the column does not match how you actually sell.
5. Is one of these three carrying most of the weight? Mark it — that is your wedge module.

## Power plays

**Job.** For each pain, summarize the GTM motion in one sentence: who you target, what trigger you watch for, what asset/play you lead with, what the desired meeting is. This row is the handoff to the Power Play Table.

**Common mistake.** Writing a campaign tagline instead of a motion ("AI Security Now" is a tagline, not a power play). Or describing the asset without the trigger and target ("we run a webinar" is half a play).

**Pressure-test questions.**
1. Does this sentence name a target ICP, a trigger, an asset, and a desired meeting? If any of the four is missing, the play is incomplete.
2. Is the trigger something you can detect — a job title change, a 10-K mention, a funding event, a tech stack signal? Or is it wishful thinking?
3. Could marketing, sales, and CS each describe their role in this play after reading the one sentence? If no, the motion is not yet operational.
4. Does this power play actually resolve the pain in the same column, or did it drift to a different pain? Power plays drift toward whichever motion the team has the most reps on.
5. If you ran this play for a quarter and it did not produce pipeline, what is the disconfirming evidence? If you cannot name it, the play has no measurable hypothesis.
